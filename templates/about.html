About Our Vehicular Traffic Noise Project

Project Overview

This traffic noise prediction and assessment project represents a comprehensive effort to understand and document the acoustic environment of an urban city [here Kanpur], one of India's major industrial and urban centers. The source from which we acquired the data has collected, analysed and interpreted data to create an accurate picture of noise exposure across different zones of the city.

Project Methodology

Data Collection Process

  • Sampling Strategy: 14 sample locations which cover the city area strategically selected across residential, commercial, industrial, and silence zones. From Feb 2019 to July 2021, data was collected for a total of 42 hours at fourteen locations that represented various land use categories on each roadway.
  • Temporal Coverage: Noise measurements were taken during three peak periods—morning (8:00-9:00 am), afternoon (12:00-1:00 pm), and evening (5:00-6:00 pm)—at a location with two lanes, resulting in 6 entries per day (3 times * 2 lanes). For each month, this gives 84 entries (14 days * 6 entries), and over 6 months, a total of 504 entries were collected.
  • Equipment: A Class 1 Sound Level Meter (SLM) with GPS was used to measure traffic noise and location. Vehicle spot speeds were recorded using a HR K-Band Handheld Radar Gun (24.125 GHz ± 100 MHz, range: 16–330 km/h stationary, 9–334 km/h typical). A Wi-Fi camera with 1080p HD, night vision, motion detection, 2-way talk, and up to 256GB storage captured traffic videos. Real-time weather data were collected using a portable weather station with a handheld display and rechargeable battery. As per IS 3028:1998 and ISO 11819-1, the SLM was placed 7.5 m from the lane centerline and 1.2 m above the pavement.
  • Measurement Duration: Noise levels were measured for a period of one hour on an A-weighted scale and reported in equivalent noise level (Leq), dB(A) units. The average traffic volume and speed for each vehicle group were also recorded.
  • Noise Maps: Noise maps depicting the variation of the noise level of Kanpur city and created by the application of QGIS Version-3.40.5 software. These noise map spatial distributions will help to find the hotspot locations of the city. A noise map depicts the shifting sound levels inside a particular geographical area over a specific time-period. Further details about noise mapping are discussed in the noise map section.

Dataset Features

Our comprehensive dataset includes:

  • Geospatial Data: GPS coordinates for each sampling location.
  • Acoustic Parameters: Sound pressure levels (dB(A)).
  • Environmental Variables: Temperature, humidity, wind speed, atmospheric pressure.
  • Contextual Information: Land use, vehicle density, average vehicle speed, Peak type.
  • Road parameters: Lane width, divider width (median), IDSS (Instrument distance same side), IDOS (Instrument distance opposite side) all in meters.

Noise Pollution: A Growing Environmental Concern

Understanding Noise Pollution

Noise pollution refers to unwanted or excessive sound that can have detrimental effects on human health and environmental quality. Unlike other forms of pollution, noise doesn't leave visible evidence but causes significant physiological and psychological impacts.

Noise is measured in decibels (dB), with higher values indicating louder sounds. The measurement is typically weighted (dB(A)) to account for the varying sensitivity of human hearing to different frequencies.

Health Impacts of Noise Exposure

Prolonged exposure to elevated noise levels has been linked to numerous health issues:

  • Auditory Effects: Temporary or permanent hearing loss, tinnitus
  • Cardiovascular Effects: Increased blood pressure, heart rate variability, risk of hypertension
  • Psychological Effects: Annoyance, stress, anxiety, depression
  • Cognitive Impairment: Reduced concentration, impaired learning, decreased work performance
  • Sleep Disturbances: Difficulty falling asleep, reduced sleep quality, daytime fatigue.

Why Kanpur? (Site Selection)

Kanpur, the largest city in Uttar Pradesh, is a major commercial and industrial hub in northern India. As per the 2011 Census, it has a population of 29.20 million and covers 3,155 sq. km. According to the Ministry of Road Transport and Highways (MoRTH), the city has 16.16 million registered vehicles.

The rise of industries and commercial sites has increased city traffic, leading to congestion and various forms of pollution, including noise. This study focused on fourteen mid-block locations with free-flowing traffic and good road conditions, categorized into four land-use zones: residential, commercial, industrial, and silent zones.

Kanpur was selected for this study due to several factors that make it particularly relevant for noise pollution research:

  • Industrial Heritage: As one of India's major industrial centers, Kanpur has numerous factories and manufacturing units contributing to the acoustic environment
  • Urban Density: High population density (approximately 6,600 people per km²) creates dense living conditions where noise propagation affects many residents
  • Transportation Hub: Major railway junction and extensive road network with heavy traffic contributing to transportation noise
  • Mixed Land Use: Close proximity of industrial, commercial, and residential areas creating complex noise patterns
  • Rapid Urbanization: Ongoing development and construction activities generating additional noise sources
  • Limited Previous Research: Despite these factors, comprehensive noise mapping studies in Kanpur have been limited

Regulatory Framework and Standards

Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) Noise Standards

The Government of India has established the following permissible noise limits for different areas:

Area Code Category of Area/Zone Limits in dB(A) Leq - Day Time Limits in dB(A) Leq - Night Time
A Industrial area 75 70
B Commercial area 65 55
C Residential area 55 45
D Silence Zone 50 40

*Silence Zones include areas within 100 meters of hospitals, educational institutions, courts, religious places, or any other area declared as such by the competent authority.

Key Project Results

  • Exceedance of Standards: Considering 14 locations on a single day, out of 42 peak times exceedance of standard noise limits was observed 30+ times.
  • Future Directions: Evidence-based policymaking for noise control in urban areas. Development of targeted noise abatement strategies for identified hotspots. Integration of noise considerations into urban planning and development.
